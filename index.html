Original Research — This Study

Research Overview

▼ Read Full Analysis

India stands at a critical inflection point. With the world's largest working-age population — over 960 million adults — the country faces a paradox of unprecedented proportions: rapid technological advancement through AI and automation threatens to displace tens of millions of workers even as the economy requires the creation of 10–12 million new jobs annually to absorb its demographic dividend.

This research project goes beyond literature review to produce original analytical contributions: two novel indices (the India AI Automation Vulnerability Index and the UBI Readiness Index), a multi-scenario fiscal simulation engine, year-by-year projections through 2040, comprehensive parliamentary intelligence mapping, and an original adaptive policy framework — the AI Adaptive Basic Income Framework (AABIF) — that dynamically links automation rates to welfare support.

The research draws on data from the Periodic Labour Force Survey 2025, Union Budget 2026-27, Economic Survey 2025-26, UIDAI Aadhaar dashboard, PMJDY statistics, World Inequality Report 2026, and over 30 primary government and institutional sources. Every major claim is supported by evidence and citations.

Key Findings

  • AI Vulnerability: Bihar, Uttar Pradesh, Jharkhand, and Rajasthan emerge as the most vulnerable states to AI-driven automation, scoring above 65 on the IAAVI index.
  • UBI Readiness: Delhi, Chandigarh, Kerala, and Goa lead UBI readiness; northeastern states and Bihar lag significantly.
  • Fiscal Reality: A minimal UBI of ₹500/month costs 1.74% of GDP; a meaningful ₹3,000/month costs 10.45% — comparable to defence spending.
  • The AABIF Solution: An adaptive framework that starts at ₹1,000/month and scales to ₹3,000+ as automation accelerates is both fiscally viable and maximally protective.
  • Scenario Winner: Full UBI with managed AI transition produces the best outcomes across all indicators by 2040 — including 3.5% unemployment, 1.5% poverty, and 7.5% GDP growth.

Phase 03 · Original Index

India AI Automation Vulnerability Index (IAAVI)

An original composite index measuring each state's vulnerability to AI-driven job displacement. 8 components, weighted, normalized 0–100. Higher score = more vulnerable.

IAAVI = Σ (Wᵢ × Nᵢ), where Wᵢ is the weight and Nᵢ is the normalized score (0–100) for component i.

All components normalized via min-max scaling across states. Higher raw values indicate greater vulnerability. Data sources: PLFS 2025, NASSCOM 2024, UIDAI, e-Shram, NSO.

Limitations: (1) Some state-level data interpolated from national aggregates; (2) BPO employment concentrated in metros, not evenly distributed within states; (3) AI readiness is an inverse composite estimate, not a direct measurement; (4) Temporal variation in informal sector definitions across surveys.

Phase 04 · Original Index

Universal Basic Income Readiness Index (UBIRI)

An original composite index measuring each state's infrastructure readiness for implementing a UBI scheme. 10 components, weighted, normalized 0–100. Higher score = more ready.

UBIRI = Σ (Wᵢ × Nᵢ), where Wᵢ is the weight and Nᵢ is the normalized score (0–100) for component i.

Poverty rate is inverted: higher poverty → lower readiness score. Data sources: UIDAI, PMJDY, TRAI, NSO, RBI, CAG reports.

Would UBI Help Minorities More Than Targeted Schemes?

▼ Read Analysis

The evidence strongly suggests that a universal approach would benefit religious minorities — particularly Muslims — more effectively than the current fragmented system of targeted minority welfare schemes. The Sachar Committee (2006) documented severe disadvantages: Muslim literacy at 59.1%, urban poverty at 38.4%, and government job representation at only 4.9%. By 2025, while some progress has occurred (literacy up to 68.5%), significant gaps persist.

The case for UBI over targeting: Targeted minority schemes suffer from three fundamental problems: (1) identification errors — the absence of a reliable minority registry means many eligible beneficiaries are excluded; (2) administrative overhead — the Ministry of Minority Affairs budget of ₹3,400 crore (2026-27) represents barely 0.06% of the Union Budget, with low utilization rates; (3) political vulnerability — minority-specific schemes face political pushback and fluctuating allocations. A universal basic income bypasses all three problems: everyone receives it, delivery is automatic via JAM Trinity, and universality provides political durability.

Verdict: Universal UBI with a benefit score of 85/100 and 95% minority reach outperforms targeted minority schemes (65/100, 70% reach) on every metric except fiscal cost.

          AI Adaptive Basic Income Framework (AABIF)

          An original adaptive welfare model that dynamically links AI automation rates to income support levels. This is the study's primary original policy contribution.

          The AABIF Formula

          AABIFTransfer = Base × (1 + αautomation × Wsector) × Rregional

          Where α = automation rate (0–1), W = sector weight, R = UBIRI-derived regional adjustment
